## Post 2: New research, Cross-Engine Consensus (BG-016)

New research from BrandGEO: "Cross-Engine Consensus, When AI Engines Agree, and When They Don't."

We tested 20 real commercial-buyer categories across 4 cities, feeding the same questions to 5 different AI engines and comparing the answers directly.

10 of the 20 categories reached a clear consensus, meaning 3 or more engines independently named the same brand. The other 10 showed no agreement at all between engines.

What separates the two groups is not random. Categories with a small number of well-documented, easily comparable products (accounting software, CRM tools, banking apps) tend to converge. Categories built on a long tail of similar local providers (employment law, independent financial advice, boutique real estate) tend to fragment.

For any brand trying to understand its own AI visibility, knowing which side of that line your category sits on changes the whole strategy.

## Post 3: Qonto and the language-category finding (Paris research)

One of the clearer results from our city research program came out of Paris.

We asked 4 AI engines "which online bank do you recommend for a French small business?" in both French and English. Every engine that gave a usable answer named the same brand first, in both languages, with no exceptions.

But a category over, in wealth management, the pattern broke completely. Ask the question in French and AI engines recommend independent boutique French advisory firms. Ask the identical question in English and they recommend major international private banks instead.

Same underlying question. Different language. A different category of business gets recommended entirely, not just a different name at the top.

For any company operating in multiple languages, this is a concrete reason to check AI visibility per language, not just per market.

*Source: BrandGEO Paris research, July 2026.*

## Post 5: Rome, when no AI engine agrees with any other

Most cities in our research program had at least one category where AI engines converged on the same recommendation. Rome didn't have a single one.

Across 8 buyer-intent categories, no category reached even 3 of 4 engines agreeing on a top pick. Instead, we found something different: individual engines being remarkably consistent with themselves, even while disagreeing with everyone else. One engine gave us the identical ranked list of restaurants, word for word, whether asked in Italian or English.

The takeaway for brands in similarly fragmented categories: getting recommended by AI isn't one project. It's closer to a separate project per engine, because each one is answering from a different, internally stable, but mutually disagreeing picture.
