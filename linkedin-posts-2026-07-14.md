## Post 2 — Qonto and the Paris finding (city-research sourced)

Here's something that surprised us in our Paris research.

We asked 4 different AI engines "which online bank do you recommend for a French small business?" in both French and English.

Every single engine that gave a usable answer named the same brand first: Qonto. In both languages. No exceptions.

That's the cleanest result we've found anywhere in our research program so far. Most categories show at least some disagreement between engines. Fintech banking in France apparently doesn't.

But the more interesting finding was one category over. We asked about wealth management advisors, and the language of the question changed which entire category of business got recommended, not just which name came out on top.

Ask in French, and engines recommend independent boutique wealth advisors. Ask the identical question in English, and they recommend major international private banks instead.

Same question. Same brand. Different answer depending on what language you typed it in.

If your business serves multilingual markets, this is worth checking for yourself.

*Source: BrandGEO Paris research, July 2026 (paris-research-findings.md).*

## Post 3 — The mechanics of AI citation (BG-article sourced, BG-009)

A question we get a lot: "does ChatGPT actually search the web before answering, or is it just guessing from memory?"

The honest answer is: sometimes.

Recent research puts the number at roughly 1 in 3 ChatGPT queries triggering a live web search, down from closer to half the queries a year earlier. And of the pages that get retrieved during that search, only about 1 in 7 actually get cited in the final answer.

That gap matters. If your brand's content isn't among the small set of pages an engine decides to trust, it doesn't matter how good your SEO is. You were never in the running.

What seems to correlate with getting picked: content updated in the last 30 days gets cited noticeably more often than older content, even when the older content is arguably more thorough.

## Post 4 — Rome: the exception that proves the rule (city-research sourced)

Every city we've researched so far has had at least one category where AI engines converge on the same answer. London had law firms. Berlin had accounting software. Paris had banking.

Rome didn't have any.

Across 8 buyer-intent prompts, not a single category reached even 3 out of 4 engines agreeing on a top pick. That's the first time that's happened in our research program.

Instead, we found something else: individual engines being remarkably consistent with themselves, even when they disagreed with everyone else. Meta AI gave us the exact same 5-restaurant list, in the exact same order, whether we asked in Italian or in English. Word for word.

So the engines aren't randomly guessing. They each have a stable internal answer. They just don't share the same one.

If you're a business in a fragmented category like this, the practical takeaway is that "getting AI to recommend you" isn't one project, it's closer to 4 or 5 separate projects, one per engine.

*Source: BrandGEO Rome research, July 2026 (rome-research-findings.md).*

## Post 5 — Freshness and citation (BG-article sourced, BG-013)

If you're wondering how often you actually need to publish to stay visible to AI search, here's a real number: content under 30 days old earns roughly 3x more AI citations than older content, even when the older content covers the same ground.

That's a much shorter cycle than most content calendars are built around.

The practical version of this isn't "publish more." It's "revisit and refresh what you already have, on a schedule shorter than you think." A 6-month-old page that gets a genuine update (new data, new examples, a corrected stat) can jump back into the window AI engines seem to weight most heavily.

## Post 6 — Consensus vs. fragmentation, the pattern across all 7 cities (research methodology, BG-016)

After running the same kind of research across London, Berlin, Madrid, New York, Paris, Rome, and Dublin, a pattern kept showing up.

Categories with a small number of well-known, easily comparable products tend to converge. CRM software, banking apps, project management tools, accounting software. AI engines land on the same 1-2 names almost every time.

Categories built on a long tail of small, similar local providers tend to fragment. Employment lawyers, independent financial advisors, boutique real estate agents. Ask 4 engines and you'll often get 4 different answers, sometimes with almost no overlap.

Out of 20 real buyer categories we tested this way, exactly half converged and half didn't.

If you're in a category that tends to fragment, that's not necessarily bad news. It usually means there's more room for a well-documented, frequently-cited brand to become the one AI engines settle on, since no one has locked that position down yet.
