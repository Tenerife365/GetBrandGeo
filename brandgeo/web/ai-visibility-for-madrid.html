AI Visibility in Madrid: Why the Same Engine Can Answer Differently in Spanish vs. English

Like Berlin, Madrid was tested bilingually — 4 categories, each asked once in Spanish and once in English, across four AI engines. But Madrid's language pattern turned out to be a different, arguably harder problem: it isn't one language consistently winning. It's engines flipping in opposite directions on the exact same question.

For one prompt, Gemini returned nothing in Spanish and a full answer in English. Meta did the reverse on that same question — a real answer in Spanish, nothing in English. Meanwhile, a completely different category (hotels near Madrid's airport) produced the most unanimous result of any city tested so far.

The language flip, in practice

For the airport-hotel question, Gemini returned nothing usable in Spanish but gave a full, real answer in English. Meta did the exact opposite on that same prompt — a real answer in Spanish, nothing in English. Both happened on the identical question, in opposite directions, for two different engines. There's no simple "AI favors English" or "AI favors the local language" rule here — coverage is genuinely inconsistent, and it's specific to the engine and the exact query, not a fixed bias you can plan around with one assumption.

One engine held steady across languages. Two didn't.

The business-dinner restaurant prompt surfaced a different kind of language pattern: not silence in one language, but a completely different set of recommendations depending on which language was asked. Meta named nearly the same shortlist of restaurants (DiverXo, Kabuki, El Prior, La Tasquita de Enfrente) in both Spanish and English. Claude and Perplexity gave two almost entirely different lists — the Spanish and English answers from the same engine barely overlapped at all. Worth noting: Meta wasn't consistently the "stable" engine across every category in this dataset — this behavior was specific to this one prompt, not a fixed trait to rely on.

A data-quality note, not a finding

ChatGPT's collection failed on every Madrid prompt this run (a technical error, `status: error`) — it isn't included in the tables above. That's not evidence ChatGPT has zero visibility for Madrid brands; it's a gap in this specific data pull that a future re-run will close.

What this means if your business is in Madrid

  1. 1

    Check both languages — don't assume one predicts the other

    This data shows an engine can go silent in one language and answer fully in the other, in either direction. If you serve both Spanish-speaking locals and English-speaking visitors or expats, your visibility in one language tells you very little about the other.

  2. 2

    If your category is unanimous, find out if you're the name it gives

    The airport-hotel category shows what near-total AI convergence looks like. A challenger brand in a category like this, absent from the answer, has a real and specific gap to close.

  3. 3

    If your category is fragmented, treat it as an open door

    Boutique hotels and business-dinner restaurants show loose or no cross-engine agreement. That's the easiest kind of category to become visible in, precisely because no incumbent has locked the field down.

  4. 4

    Re-run this regularly, not once

    These answers reflect what the engines say today, in each language, this week. As this page shows, an engine's language coverage can flip between runs — a check from months ago won't tell you what's true now.

● Common Questions

Does an AI engine always favor English over the local language, or vice versa?

No — Madrid's data shows it can go either way, unpredictably, even within the same engine. For the airport-hotel question, Gemini returned nothing in Spanish but a full answer in English. Meta did the exact opposite on the identical question — a real answer in Spanish, nothing in English. There's no simple rule that one language always wins.

Which category showed the strongest AI consensus in Madrid?

Hotels near Madrid-Barajas airport, by a wide margin. Hilton Madrid Airport was named by every engine that returned a usable result, in both Spanish and English. That's the pattern seen elsewhere in this research program too: well-documented, internationally recognizable brands (airport hotel chains, major software products) converge across engines, while smaller, independent, locally-known businesses fragment.

Why is ChatGPT missing from this data?

ChatGPT's collection failed on every Madrid prompt this run — a technical error, not evidence ChatGPT has zero visibility for Madrid brands. This page reflects the 4 engines that returned usable results: Claude, Gemini, Meta AI, and Perplexity. A future re-run will include ChatGPT once the collection succeeds.
