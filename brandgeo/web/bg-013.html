How Often You Need to Publish to Stay Cited by AI

AI engines reward recent content — but not evenly across content types. Here's the actual refresh cadence, and when "old" genuinely stops mattering.

The direct answer

It depends on what the page is for, and the gap between content types is bigger than most publishing calendars account for. Comparison, ranking, and pricing-shaped content needs a real refresh every 60-90 days to stay eligible for citation. Evergreen how-to and pillar guides can run on a 6-month cycle. Pure reference and definition content — the kind that answers "what is X" rather than "what's the best X right now" — can often go a full year between updates and still hold its citation share, provided the underlying facts haven't actually changed. Treating all three the same way is the most common mistake: it either burns effort refreshing pages that didn't need it, or leaves fast-decaying commercial pages stale for months at a time.

Why freshness is a mechanical requirement, not a ranking preference

Every answer from ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, or Claude that cites a source is built the same way under the hood: retrieve a set of candidate documents first, then generate an answer grounded in whatever was retrieved. Freshness gets applied as a filter at that retrieval step, not as a stylistic nudge afterward — when two sources cover the same claim, the system preferentially pulls the newer one into the candidate set before generation even starts. That's a structural difference from classic search ranking, where an old, well-linked page can still win a position indefinitely.

This isn't a theory. A researcher who reverse-engineered ChatGPT's production search configuration found use_freshness_scoring_profile: true set directly in its reranker settings — freshness isn't incidental, it's a named, deliberately tuned parameter in the pipeline. GrowByData's 2026 study of ChatGPT citation patterns quantified the effect: content updated within the last 30 days earns 3.2x more citations than older pages on the same topic, and 76.4% of ChatGPT's most-cited pages had been updated within that same 30-day window.

Perplexity runs an even more time-sensitive version of the same mechanic, and it does so through two separate systems: PerplexityBot continuously refreshes the index behind cited answers, while a second live-fetch layer pulls pages in real time for a subset of queries. The result is a sharper cliff than ChatGPT's — content published in the last 30 days captures 82% of Perplexity's citations for a given topic, and that share falls to just 37% once a page crosses six months old. Claude is the outlier worth knowing about: per the same research, it weights recency relatively lightly compared to the other three engines, which matters for how you prioritize a refresh queue if Claude visibility is a real part of your mix.

The mechanism in one line

Freshness isn't a tiebreaker applied after an AI engine decides what's good. It's a filter applied before the engine ever sees your content as a candidate to cite.

A practical cadence, by content type — not a blanket rule

Applying one refresh schedule to an entire site gets the incentive backwards, because different content types decay at genuinely different rates. The table below is the cadence that a real refresh program should run on, reconciling the 30/90/180-day cliffs above with how differently each content type actually ages.

The underlying logic tracks the mechanism above: fast-moving, comparison-shaped content is exactly what Perplexity's 82%-to-37% cliff and GrowByData's 3.2x multiplier were measured on, while reference content behaves closer to the pattern BG-001's six discipline definitions are built around — content whose value comes from being correct, not from being new.

Does old, authoritative content ever get a pass?

Partially, and it's worth being precise about where the line actually falls rather than treating "freshness matters" as absolute. Gander's 2026 half-life study — measuring the internal retrieval layer across 194,077 sources — found that AI retrieval visibility drops roughly 40-60% for every year a page ages, which sounds like a hard ceiling on anything old. But that number is an average across all content types, and it masks a real split underneath: Ahrefs' own analysis of evergreen content in AI search found that a two-year-old definition page can still earn citations if its claims are independently corroborated by newer sources, while a two-year-old market analysis or vendor comparison is, in their words, effectively invisible to AI retrieval. The decay curve isn't one curve — it's steep for time-anchored claims and much gentler for stable, corroborated facts.

There's a second nuance worth knowing before you build a refresh calendar around any of this: AI systems can tell the difference between a real update and a cosmetic one. Swapping "2025" for "2026" in a title without changing anything underneath doesn't register as freshness signal — the content itself, not just the timestamp, is what gets re-evaluated. That's the same distinction BG-007 already drew when it found that 50% of AI-cited content is under 13 weeks old: the win goes to content that's genuinely been reworked — new data, corrected claims, an added section — not to whichever page's metadata was touched most recently.

Practically, this means a page like BG-005's GEO-vs-SEO breakdown is a reasonable candidate to age gracefully on something closer to the 12-month cycle, provided the core distinction it draws stays accurate — while a page ranking specific vendors or naming current pricing has no such grace period, regardless of how well-written or well-linked it is.

What to actually do with this

  • Split your content inventory into the three tiers above before scheduling anything. A single "refresh everything quarterly" policy either wastes effort on reference pages that don't need it or leaves your highest-decay pages stale for months.
  • Refresh means new substance, not a new date. Update the actual data, add a section that reflects something that's changed, correct a claim — a timestamp change alone doesn't move any of the citation numbers above.
  • Put comparison and pricing content on the tightest cycle you can sustain. This is the category with the steepest measured decay in every study cited here.
  • Don't over-invest in refreshing genuinely stable reference content. Per the Ahrefs finding above, a well-corroborated definitional page can hold its citation share well past a year — that effort is better spent on the pages actually decaying.
  • Track which of your pages are actually getting cited, and by which engine, before deciding what to refresh next. Claude's lighter recency weighting versus Perplexity's 180-day cliff means the "right" refresh priority can differ by which engines matter most to your brand — a decision BG-003 covers in more depth for ChatGPT specifically.
