How ChatGPT Actually Decides Which Brands to Recommend

Most ChatGPT answers never touch the live web at all. Here's what happens on the query that does — and the query that doesn't.

The direct answer

ChatGPT doesn't evaluate every brand recommendation live. Most of the time, it doesn't search the web at all — it answers from what it already learned during training, which means the brands it names are whatever names showed up most, and most positively, in that training data. Only when a query needs something training data can't supply does ChatGPT open its web_search_preview tool, retrieve a batch of live pages, and cite a small fraction of what it read. Two genuinely different processes produce a "ChatGPT recommended you" outcome, and they reward almost entirely different things — which is the part most brand-visibility advice skips.

Does ChatGPT even search the web for your recommendation query?

Per OpenAI's own developer documentation, the web_search_preview and web_search tools support two distinct modes. Non-reasoning search sends the query straight to the search tool and passes along whatever comes back — fast, and with no real planning behind it. Agentic search, used by reasoning models, actively manages the process: it runs searches as part of its own chain of thought, reads what comes back, and decides whether to search again before answering. Either way, search is a tool the model chooses to invoke, not a step every query passes through.

That choice is selective, and it's trending more selective over time. Per Semrush's 17-month clickstream analysis of ChatGPT traffic, the web search feature was enabled on just 34.5% of queries as of February 2026 — down from 46% in late 2024. In practice, search tends to trigger for anything time-sensitive or fact-dependent — "who's the best X right now," current pricing, recent reviews — and stays off for queries a model could have answered the same way two years ago, which it answers from parametric memory alone.

The stakes of that decision are real. OpenAI's own GPT-5 evaluation data, cross-checked against an independent peer-reviewed analysis published in a medical-writing journal, shows the model's hallucination rate on fact-seeking tasks at roughly 47% with browsing disabled, dropping to about 9.6% with browsing enabled — a reduction bigger than switching to an entirely different model. A brand named in a no-browsing answer is a brand the model is recalling, not verifying.

The retrieval-to-citation funnel: what happens once it does search

When search does trigger, most of what gets retrieved never makes it into the answer. AirOps analyzed 548,534 pages retrieved across 15,000 original prompts — a set that expanded to 43,233 queries once ChatGPT's own follow-up "fan-out" searches were counted, since 89.6% of prompts triggered two or more sub-searches. Of everything retrieved across that whole process, ChatGPT cited only 15%. The other 85% was pulled in, evaluated against the query, and discarded.

Ranking well on Google helps, but it isn't close to sufficient. The same study found pages ranked #1 on Google get cited by ChatGPT 43.2% of the time — 3.5x more often than pages outside the top 20 — which still means fewer than half of the single best-ranked pages for a query end up cited. And nearly a third of all citations (32.9%) came from ChatGPT's own fan-out queries, not the prompt's original keywords — meaning a page can be irrelevant to what the user literally typed and still get cited, if it answers the sub-question ChatGPT generated on its own.

What ChatGPT actually trusts once it looks

Two signals dominate what survives that 15% cut: where the content lives, and how recently it changed. On source type, per Muck Rack's May 2026 analysis of over 25 million cited links across ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini spanning 17 industries, earned, third-party media accounts for 84% of all AI citations — a figure that lines up closely with University of Toronto researchers' independent controlled experiments across AI search platforms, which put the earned-media share at 82-89%. Wikipedia, LinkedIn, Reddit, and established editorial domains dominate the source pool ChatGPT actually draws from; brand-owned pages are structurally underrepresented relative to how much brands invest in them.

On recency, per ConvertMate's analysis of ChatGPT citation patterns, 76.4% of ChatGPT citations come from content updated within the last 30 days — the strongest recency preference measured of any major AI engine when cross-checked against Ahrefs' separate 17-million-citation freshness study. A page's age matters less than when it was last meaningfully touched.

The reframe

Once ChatGPT decides to look, it isn't asking "who ranks well." It's asking "who do independent sources vouch for, recently." That's a different brief than most SEO programs are built around.

The open question: live evaluation, or incumbent bias?

This is the point where the research genuinely splits, and it's worth stating both sides rather than picking the one that sells better. One camp — most GEO advice, including much of what's above — treats ChatGPT's recommendations as the output of a live evaluation: retrieve, filter, weigh authority and freshness, cite the winners. That model is real and well-documented for the 34.5% of queries that actually search.

The other camp points at what happens the other 65.5% of the time. A 2026 academic paper on "Incumbent Advantage" in LLM recommendation systems documents a phenomenon its authors call the Conditional Monopoly: when compared options are roughly equivalent, well-known brands get recommended close to 100% of the time — not because a live signal favored them, but because they're simply better-represented, more positively, in the training data. That dominance can evaporate the moment a competitor gets even a marginal edge (the paper cites a swing from a small rating advantage), which suggests the bias is a soft default rather than a hard lock — but a default is still what most no-search answers are running on. Separate bias research adds a related wrinkle: LLMs are tuned to avoid recommending anything that reads as controversial or unverified, which mechanically favors brands already large enough to be "safe," independent of whether a smaller competitor is actually better.

Neither camp is wrong. They're describing two different code paths inside the same product. The honest takeaway is that ChatGPT visibility work has to hit both: earning the live-retrieval signals above for the third of queries that search, while accepting that the other two-thirds are shaped by whatever's already in the training corpus about your brand — which is a slower-moving, much harder lever to pull, and the reason a single strong month of citations doesn't fix a brand's baseline recall the way a month of link-building used to move a Google ranking.

The mechanics above run on a different pipeline than the other two engines this research has already mapped. BG-006 covers Perplexity's RAG process — which evaluates 8-10 candidate sources per query on every single query, no on/off switch — and Gemini's grounding classifier, which decides per-query whether to run a live search at all, much closer to ChatGPT's own selective behavior than Perplexity's is. If you're building an AI-visibility strategy around a single engine's mechanics, it likely won't transfer to the next one.

What this means for your brand

Two separate problems need two separate fixes, and most brands are only working on one of them. For the third of queries that trigger a live search, the earned-media and freshness signals above are the actual scoreboard — see BG-007 for the fuller data on which specific signals correlate most with getting cited once retrieval happens. For the other two-thirds, answered from training data alone, the lever is slower: consistent, positive, independently-sourced mentions accumulated over time, since that's the material the next training run — or the next fine-tune — actually learns from. A brand that only exists in a well-optimized way on its own domain, chasing the live-search signals, is invisible to the much larger share of ChatGPT answers that never search in the first place.
