AI Visibility in London: What ChatGPT, Gemini & Perplexity Actually Recommend

We didn't guess at this one. BrandGEO ran 8 real commercial-buyer prompts — the exact kind of question a Londoner types when they need a lawyer, a CRM, or a wealth manager — through its own collection pipeline against four major AI engines, then compared every answer side by side.

Some categories turned out to be completely unanimous: every engine names the same handful of brands. Others are wide open, with every engine naming an entirely different set of firms and no agreement at all. Both are useful signals — one tells you who currently owns the AI narrative, the other tells you the field is still up for grabs.

Why this split matters

Fintech, CRM, and project-management tools have a small number of well-documented, frequently-reviewed products — so every engine converges on the same names. Employment law and independent financial advice have thousands of qualified firms with thin or inconsistent footprints across the sources AI engines actually draw from — so each engine names a completely different set. In categories like these, no one currently owns the AI narrative, which means the field is still genuinely open for whichever firm gets its structured data, directory presence, and citable content in order first.

Engines don't all recommend the same kind of brand

Beyond raw agreement, the type of brand each engine favors differs in ways that matter if you're trying to be one of them. Perplexity leaned toward claimant-side and directory-style answers (Leigh Day, Thompsons, Unbiased, VouchedFor) where Claude and Meta AI leaned toward established City firms. On property, Claude's list of solicitors was entirely disjoint from the other three engines' — smaller high-street conveyancing names on one side, prime-property specialists like Farrer & Co and Forsters on the other. A brand can be highly visible on one engine and completely invisible on another, which is exactly why checking a single engine tells you far less than checking all of them.

What this means if your business is in one of these categories

  1. 1

    If your category is unanimous, find out if you're one of the names

    In categories like CRM, PM tools, and fintech banking, AI engines have already converged on a short list. If you're a challenger brand in one of these spaces, being absent from that list is a real, measurable gap — not a hypothetical one.

  2. 2

    If your category is fragmented, treat it as an open door

    Employment law and independent financial advice show no cross-engine agreement at all right now. That's the easiest kind of category to become visible in, because there's no incumbent the AI engines have already settled on.

  3. 3

    Check more than one engine

    The property-solicitor results above show a brand can dominate one engine's answers and be entirely missing from another's. A single-engine check (or worse, just checking Google) will miss that split completely.

  4. 4

    Re-run this regularly, not once

    These answers reflect what the engines say today. AI-generated recommendations shift as engines re-crawl directories, reviews, and press coverage — a snapshot from six months ago won't tell you what's true now.

● Common Questions

Is this real data, or a hypothetical example?

It's real. BrandGEO ran 8 actual commercial-buyer prompts ("best CRM software for a small business in London," "best independent financial advisors in London," and similar) through its own collection pipeline against Claude, Gemini, Meta AI, and Perplexity, then compared what each engine actually answered. Nothing here is simulated or estimated — it's a direct read of what these engines say today.

Why do some categories have a clear AI-recommended leader and others don't?

It comes down to how much consistent, citable information exists about brands in that category across the sources AI engines draw from. Categories like fintech banking apps and CRM software have a small number of well-documented, frequently-reviewed products, so every engine converges on the same names. Categories like employment law and independent financial advice have thousands of qualified firms with thin, inconsistent online footprints, so each engine ends up naming a different set — there's no consensus because there's no dominant signal for any engine to latch onto.

What does it mean if my business isn't named in any of these results?

These 8 prompts only cover a handful of categories — not naming a specific business here doesn't mean it's invisible everywhere. But if your category is one of the fragmented ones (employment law, independent financial advice), it's worth checking whether your own brand shows up at all when these exact questions are asked, since the data shows the field is genuinely wide open in categories like these right now.
