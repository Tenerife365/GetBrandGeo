AI Visibility in Miami: Immigration Law Breaks the Pattern

We ran 6 real Miami categories (personal injury law, real estate, property management, immigration law, international business law, and hotels/hospitality) through the same AI Visibility pipeline BrandGEO runs for paying clients, across five AI engines. The headline: Miami is only the second city in this program where immigration law showed strong AI consensus instead of fragmenting, tying property management as the city's strongest category.

International business law showed solid mid-tier consensus, directly reflecting Miami's role as the "Gateway to the Americas." Individual real estate brokers, meanwhile, stayed fragmented, though the Compass brand showed up under three different local team names.

Immigration law breaks its own pattern, for the second time

In New York, Los Angeles, Houston, and Dallas, immigration law was consistently one of the most fragmented categories, dominated by individually-rated practitioners. In Miami, Kurzban Kurzban Tetzeli & Pratt reached 4 of 5 engines, named #1 by ChatGPT's prose, Claude, and Gemini, and listed by name in Google AI Mode. Chicago was the first city where this category converged; Miami is the second.

One brand, three local names

No individual real estate broker or team crossed 2 of 5 engines in Miami, on the surface, the most fragmented category in the dataset. But look closer: ChatGPT named "Compass Miami," Google AI Mode named "Compass Florida," and Claude named "The Carroll Group (Compass)", three different labels for the same underlying brokerage brand, appearing across 3 of the 5 engines. The same institutions-over-individuals pattern already seen in New York and Los Angeles, just wearing a local team name in each engine's answer.

A data-quality note, not a finding

All 5 engines returned usable data on every prompt this run, no collection errors. One genuine engine-interpretation quirk: asked for "hotels and hospitality management companies," 4 of the 5 engines answered almost entirely with hotel properties rather than actual management firms, only Gemini (Sound Hospitality Management) and Google AI Mode (AIC Hotel Group) named genuine management companies. Kept as reported since every named business is real, similar to a real-estate/relocation blending quirk seen in the Dallas dataset. This run also used Google AI Mode in place of the now-retired Meta AI engine used in earlier city pages.

What this means if your business is in Miami

  1. 1

    If you're in immigration law, the bar just got higher

    Unlike most cities tested, Miami shows real cross-engine consensus on a single immigration firm. A competitor absent from that answer faces a genuine, documented gap, not just general low visibility.

  2. 2

    If you're an independent broker, know you're competing with a brand, not just other agents

    Compass shows up under three different local names across three engines. An independent broker isn't just competing with other individuals, they're competing with an institutional brand that gets named regardless of which specific team answers the query.

  3. 3

    Check whether AI engines actually understand your category

    Engines answered a hospitality-management prompt with hotel names instead. If your business sits in a category that AI models could misread, verify what they're actually recommending when someone asks.

  4. 4

    Re-run this regularly, not once

    These answers reflect what the engines say today, this week. Engine behavior and extraction quality both shift between runs. A one-time check tells you less than an ongoing one.

● Common Questions

Which brand had the strongest AI consensus in Miami?

Two brands tied at 4 of 5 engines: Pristine Property Management for property management, and Kurzban Kurzban Tetzeli & Pratt for immigration law.

Did any AI engines misinterpret a Miami category prompt?

Yes. Asked for "hotels and hospitality management companies," 4 of the 5 engines answered almost entirely with hotel properties rather than actual management companies, a genuine prompt-interpretation quirk, similar to a real-estate/relocation mix-up seen in the Dallas dataset.
