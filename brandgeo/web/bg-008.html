AI Visibility by Industry: Who Wins, Who Loses

Which industries are most exposed to AI disruption in search — and which sectors already have strong AI Visibility without knowing it.

AI search isn't hitting every industry the same way

The honest, direct answer to "which industries are winning and losing in AI search" is: it depends whether you mean traffic, citation opportunity, or the gap between the two — because those three things diverge sharply by sector, and the industries you'd assume are struggling most aren't always the ones the data actually points to. B2B SaaS companies are losing real, measurable traffic. Local businesses have been quietly locked out of AI recommendations almost entirely, despite looking fine by every classic local-SEO metric. And a small number of industries — healthcare chief among them — are structured in a way where existing SEO strength genuinely does carry over into AI citation, which most brands in those categories haven't yet realized.

The clearest losers: B2B SaaS and content-led businesses

This is the one category where the damage is unambiguous and already reflected in traffic numbers, not just citation-share estimates. Per The Digital Bloom's 2026 Organic Traffic Crisis Report, HubSpot — long considered an SEO benchmark for the category — saw its organic traffic fall roughly 70-80% between November 2024 and Q2 2025 as AI Overviews scaled up, with monthly organic visits reportedly dropping from around 13.5 million to under 7 million in a matter of weeks. That's the headline case, but it's not an outlier: a broader study from Onely and ABM Agency found B2B websites saw an average 34% year-over-year organic traffic decline, and that B2B research-style queries specifically lose 22% of their organic traffic once an AI Overview appears.

The nuance worth holding onto: not every B2B SaaS query type is hit equally. The same research found SaaS comparison queries — the "X vs Y" pages further down the funnel, closer to a buying decision — retain 71% of their pre-AI-Overview click-through rate, because commercial intent still pulls users onto a product page. The traffic that's disappearing fastest is the top-of-funnel "what is" and "how to" content B2B marketing teams built entire strategies around — the exact content type AI engines are best equipped to fully answer inline, with no reason left for the reader to click through.

The illusion: local businesses that assume they're visible

This is the category most likely to catch a brand off guard, because the metric that used to signal "we're visible" doesn't transfer. Per SOCi's 2026 Local Visibility Index — an audit of 350,000+ business locations across 2,751 brands — ChatGPT recommends just 1.2% of local business locations, meaning 98.8% are effectively invisible to it. Compare that to Google's classic local 3-pack, where brands in the same dataset appeared 35.9% of the time. Gemini and Perplexity are somewhat less selective (11% and 7.4% recommendation rates respectively), but still nowhere close to matching classic local-pack visibility. The same report found only 45% overlap between the top 20 brands by traditional local search visibility and the top 20 brands AI engines actually recommend — meaning more than half of the businesses winning Google's map pack are simply absent from AI answers for the same searches.

We've already seen this exact gap up close: in BG-004, a Bucharest catering brand ranked #1 across three separate AI engines and had a page-one Google presence to match — and was completely absent on a fourth. That's this industry-wide pattern showing up in one real audit. A strong Google Business Profile and a page-one local ranking are no longer evidence that AI engines have found you; they're evidence that a different, older system has.

The metric that stopped working

A local business owner checking their Google ranking today is checking the wrong signal. 98.8% invisibility to AI recommendations coexists comfortably with a strong local pack position — the two are now measuring almost entirely different things.

The real winners: industries where SEO strength actually transfers

A smaller set of industries sit in a genuinely different position — not because AI search spares them, but because the underlying signals AI citation rewards happen to already be strong in those categories, whether or not anyone deliberately built for it. Per Siftly's 2026 AI Citation Rate Benchmarks, healthcare shows the highest AI-citation overlap with existing organic top-10 rankings of any industry measured, at roughly 24% — meaning Google's trust signals for healthcare content (author credentials, medical review processes, established domain authority) are more tightly coupled to AI citation eligibility than in almost any other sector. A healthcare brand that's already invested in the classic authority signals search engines have long rewarded is, mechanically, closer to AI-visible than a comparable brand in a lower-overlap category doing the same SEO work.

Education and B2B tech content show a related pattern from a different angle: per ALM Corp's 2026 industry tracking, education queries now trigger an AI Overview 83% of the time, up from just 18% a year earlier, and B2B tech climbed from 36% to 82% over the same period. High AI Overview presence cuts both ways — more exposure to zero-click loss, but also more surface area for a well-optimized brand to actually be the source cited inside that Overview. E-commerce sits at the opposite extreme by design: Google has deliberately scaled back AI Overview presence on transactional queries, from roughly 29% down to about 4%, to protect ad revenue on purchase-intent searches — which caps e-commerce's AI citation opportunity but also limits how much organic traffic it stands to lose to summarization in the first place.

What this means for your brand

The industry you're in changes what "AI Visibility risk" actually means, but it doesn't remove it. If you're in B2B SaaS, the fire is already real and already measurable in your traffic numbers. If you're a local or service business, the fire hasn't been noticed yet, because the metric everyone's still checking — Google's local pack — isn't the one that predicts AI recommendation anymore. And if you're in healthcare, education, or B2B tech, the same high-exposure AI Overview environment that threatens weaker competitors is a real opportunity if your content is the one actually getting cited. None of these is knowable from a classic SEO dashboard. It only shows up when you check the AI engines directly, against your own real customer questions.
