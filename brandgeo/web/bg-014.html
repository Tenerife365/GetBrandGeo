Why Your Brand Might Be Invisible to AI Outside English

Most AI-visibility tooling — and most of the discourse around it — is built and tested almost entirely in English. Here's what that structurally costs a brand the moment a real customer asks in their own language.

The direct answer: yes, and it's structural, not incidental

If your brand operates in a market where customers don't default to English, there is a real, measurable chance that AI engines are less likely to know you, cite you, or recommend you than an English-language audit of your AI Visibility would suggest. This isn't a minor localization gap or a matter of translating your website. It's a consequence of how the models themselves were built: trained overwhelmingly on English text, tuned and benchmarked overwhelmingly in English, and — for the engines that ground answers in live search — often drawing from a thinner, differently-shaped pool of sources the moment the query itself isn't in English. None of this is a flaw unique to one AI provider. It's close to universal across the category, which is exactly why it's worth understanding rather than assuming away.

Why English dominates: the training-data mechanic

The starting point is training-data composition, and the numbers are stark even for open, well-documented models. Meta's own published breakdown of Llama 2's pretraining corpus shows 89.7% English text, with every other individual language making up less than 2% of the total — the remainder split between code and a long tail of other languages, each getting a sliver. Newer models have generally improved this ratio, and 2026 research into multilingual data mixtures shows the field shifting toward more deliberately balanced training sets. But the underlying imbalance hasn't disappeared — it's shrunk. English remains what researchers call the "pivot language": the language a model reasons in most fluently, because it's the language it saw the most of, from the highest-quality and most diverse sources, by a wide margin.

This matters for AI Visibility specifically because it's the same mechanism behind the six disciplines we mapped in BG-001 — GEO, AEO, and the rest all assume the underlying model has enough signal about your brand to work with. A brand with a strong, well-documented English-language footprint is handing the model exactly the kind of dense, high-confidence training signal it's best equipped to use. A brand whose entire footprint is in Romanian, Polish, or Vietnamese is asking the model to work from a dramatically thinner slice of what it learned from.

What actually happens when the question isn't asked in English

There are two separate mechanisms at play here, and they compound rather than cancel out.

The first is reasoning quality. Recent research into cross-lingual model behavior found that 70-85% of the accuracy gap in lower-resource languages comes down to the model's inability to map a non-English prompt into its dominant, English-centric "reasoning space" — not a lack of surface-level vocabulary, but a genuine difference in how reliably the model can think through the answer. Some engines handle this by translating internally before reasoning and translating back before responding; others reason more natively in the query's own language. Neither path is free: internal translation is a lossy round trip that can blur nuance, drop context, or lose a brand name's exact form, while native-language reasoning in a lower-resource language is working with a thinner training signal to begin with, per the training-data point above.

The second mechanism is which sources get consulted at all — directly relevant to the sourcing question BG-003 already covered for English-language queries. A 2026 Profound study spanning 3.25 billion AI citations across 7 models and 14 countries found that query language reshapes citation behavior on its own, independent of the country the user is in — the same question, asked in Spanish instead of English, saw TikTok's share of ChatGPT's citations jump roughly 5x, while other languages produced entirely different source mixes again. If the pool of sources an engine draws from shifts that dramatically by language, a brand that's only built its citation-worthy footprint in English is invisible to whichever different pool gets consulted the moment someone asks in their own language.

The reframe that matters

An AI engine doesn't have one opinion of your brand that it expresses in different languages. It effectively re-derives a separate, differently-informed answer for each language a customer might ask in — built from a different slice of training data and, often, a different pool of live sources.

What this means if your customers don't search in English

The practical risk isn't hypothetical, and it's not limited to obviously "foreign" markets — it applies to any brand whose real customers ask questions in a language other than English, which describes the large majority of the world's buyers. Three implications follow directly from the mechanics above.

First, an English-language AI Visibility audit tells you almost nothing about how you're doing in the languages your actual customers use. We've already documented this exact gap concretely: in BG-004, a Bucharest catering brand ranked #1 across three AI engines and was completely absent on a fourth — a citation gap that showed up purely from asking the same category of question and comparing engines. The same kind of gap opens up, often more dramatically, when the variable being changed is the query's language instead of the engine.

Second, this compounds with the industry pattern in BG-008: local and regional businesses already sit in the weakest position for AI recommendation relative to their classic search visibility, and a non-English market adds a second, independent layer of disadvantage on top of the first. A locally strong brand with excellent Google rankings in its home market can be simultaneously invisible to AI recommendations for the reasons BG-008 covers, and additionally under-cited relative to a similarly-positioned English-market brand, for the reasons this article covers.

Third — and this is the part worth acting on, not just noting — translating or localizing content measurably helps. Analysis of over 1.3 million AI Overview citations found properly localized sites earn 327% more visibility in AI Overviews than single-language equivalents. That's not a small optimization; it's evidence that the gap this article describes is addressable, not fixed. The brands losing out aren't losing to a law of physics — they're losing to the fact that almost nobody has built a native-language footprint deliberately, because almost no AI-visibility tooling checks for it.

How BrandGEO treats this differently

Most AI-visibility monitoring tools — including most of the direct competitors we've researched for this site — are built, tested, and marketed around English-language prompts, sometimes with a translation layer bolted on afterward. That approach measures whether your brand shows up when someone asks in English. It doesn't measure whether your brand shows up when someone asks in the language your actual customer used.

BrandGEO's collection pipeline injects real market and region context into every prompt it runs — not a generic "worldwide" default, but the specific market a client operates in, so a query gets asked the way a real local customer would ask it, not translated after the fact from an English template. On the analysis side, sentiment scoring is the clearest place this matters in practice: a keyword-based sentiment model built from an English word list will misread genuine praise written in another language, or worse, silently score it as neutral because none of the "positive" words it knows how to recognize appear. BrandGEO maintains a dedicated Romanian sentiment lexicon, built and expanded directly from real client response data rather than a direct translation of English terms — the kind of language-specific groundwork that a bolted-on translation layer doesn't do, because it isn't designed to catch how praise, hedging, or criticism actually gets phrased in a given language.

We're not going to overstate this: multilingual AI-citation measurement is genuinely unfinished work for everyone in this category, BrandGEO included — diacritic handling, dialect variation, and lower-resource languages all remain areas of active improvement, not solved problems. But the starting posture is the difference. A brand-visibility tool has to be built to take language seriously from the ground up, not patched afterward, to have any chance of telling you the truth about a market that doesn't run on English.
