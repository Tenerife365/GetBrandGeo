A yes/no mention check tells you almost nothing about where you stand. Here's the fuller measurement framework — and where a spreadsheet stops being enough.

Why "were we mentioned?" is the wrong first question

The direct answer: mention rate is the floor of AI-visibility measurement, not the ceiling. Knowing you were named in an AI answer says nothing about where you landed in a ranked list, how you were framed, whether a competitor got more airtime in the same response, or whether the same prompt would still name you if you asked again tomorrow. A 2026 methodology comparison from LLM Pulse makes the size of that gap concrete: run the identical dataset for the same brand through three defensible scoring methods and you get three different stories — 20% by simple mention count, 16.8% by position-weighted share, and 31.4% by citation-based share. None of the three numbers is wrong. All three are incomplete on their own, which is exactly the trap a single "mentioned: yes" checkbox sets.

We've already made the narrower version of this case in BG-012 — a mention and a genuinely useful visibility signal are related, but they aren't interchangeable, and treating them as the same thing is how a brand ends up confidently reporting a number that only tells half the story. This piece is the fuller version: the complete set of dimensions worth tracking once "were we mentioned" stops being a satisfying answer, how to start tracking them without buying anything, and the point at which a spreadsheet genuinely can't keep up.

The seven KPIs mention-count can't see

Every AI-visibility platform on the market — including Peec AI's own core dashboard, built around visibility, position, and sentiment as its three founding metrics — converges on roughly the same starting set. That convergence is itself a signal worth trusting: several vendors built competing products from scratch and landed in the same place, which is a reasonable minimum for any team measuring this by hand to start from too. Consistency is its own KPI, not an afterthought

The last two rows in that table surprise people most, and they're worth dwelling on because they change how often you need to measure, not just what. Per 2026 per-engine research on citation overlap, the domains ChatGPT cites for a given category and the domains Perplexity cites for the identical prompts overlap by only about 11%. Track one engine and you're not sampling a smaller version of the full picture — you're looking at a mostly different picture that happens to share a category. BG-007 goes deeper on why that split exists: engines weight mention volume, freshness, and cross-source verification differently, so the same underlying reality about your brand produces genuinely different answers depending on which one is asked.

The time dimension is just as unstable. LLM Pulse's 2026 GEO measurement research found only around 30% of brands remain visible between two consecutive runs of the same query, and cited domain sets in active categories drift 40-60% month over month. A single audit, however carefully run, is a snapshot of a system that keeps moving. That's the practical argument for a cadence, not a one-time check: the same research calls a 100-200 prompt panel, run weekly, the minimum viable system for anything you'd call reliable.

The reframe that matters

A "yes, we were mentioned" from three weeks ago isn't a status — it's a data point from a system that has since moved on. Consistency across engines and consistency over time are measurements in their own right, not footnotes to the ones above them.

Building a simple tracking system without dedicated tooling

None of this requires a platform to start. A workable manual system fits in a spreadsheet:

  • Build a panel of 20-50 real prompts a customer would actually type — the underlying question ("best project management tool for a 10-person agency"), not your brand name.
  • Run the same panel across at least two engines — given the ~11% cross-engine overlap above, one engine alone isn't measuring the category, it's measuring that engine.
  • Log five things per response: mentioned (yes/no), position if listed, a one-line sentiment call, which competitors also appeared, and which sources the engine visibly cited.
  • Repeat on a fixed cadence — weekly, not whenever someone remembers — since a single run tells you almost nothing on its own, per the consistency figures above.
  • Read the trend, not any single week's number, before drawing a conclusion about whether you're actually improving.

This gets real signal, and it's the same discipline BG-003 maps out in more depth: the six-stage path a prompt travels before an engine actually names a brand is the exact path your manual panel is sampling — just by hand, instead of at scale.

When a spreadsheet stops being enough

The manual version breaks down in three predictable ways. It doesn't scale — two engines and 20 prompts weekly is roughly 40 manual chat sessions a month, and a real category needs more than that. It isn't consistent — sentiment and position calls made by whoever happened to be logging that week drift, with no way to flag when the methodology itself has quietly shifted. And it can't see the traffic side of the story at all: per Clickport's April 2026 analysis, corroborated by The Digital Bloom's own traffic-share research, roughly 70% of AI referral traffic arrives with no referrer header at all, invisible to a standard Google Analytics setup — so even a perfect manual citation log tells you nothing about whether any of it is actually converting.

That's the point most teams graduate to a dedicated platform — not because manual tracking is wrong, but because it hits a real ceiling. It's also, not coincidentally, where the market itself already sits: Peec AI, Profound, Otterly, and AthenaHQ all build on the same visibility/position/sentiment core, then add what a spreadsheet structurally can't — automated multi-engine polling on a fixed cadence, citation-source intelligence, and, on the more built-out platforms, a direct line from a visibility gap to a recommended fix.

What this means for your brand

Start wherever you are. If you've never checked past a single "did ChatGPT mention us" query, the spreadsheet version above is a real improvement over nothing, and it's genuinely enough for a team just getting oriented. If you're already running that panel weekly and it's consuming more of someone's time than the insight is worth — or leadership is asking for a trend line a spreadsheet can't produce — that's the actual signal to graduate, not a fixed budget or headcount threshold. Either way, the number worth retiring as your primary KPI is the one this article opened with: mentioned, yes or no.
