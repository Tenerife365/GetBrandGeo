AI Visibility for Law Firms: How to Get Recommended by ChatGPT, Gemini & Perplexity

Before someone ever searches Google for "divorce lawyer near me," a growing number of them are asking ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity the same question first — and getting a named answer. If your firm isn't one of the names an AI engine gives back, you're not in the running before the prospective client even opens a search bar.

Being the answer an AI engine recommends is now a real client-acquisition channel for law firms — especially solo practitioners and regional firms competing for local, high-intent searches like "do I need a lawyer for X" or "best [practice area] lawyer in [city]." This guide covers what people actually ask AI engines about lawyers, what the current data says about how visible law firms are, and a practical checklist for improving it.

How to improve your AI Visibility as a law firm

AI engines don't rank pages the way Google does. Most pull from a narrow set of signals — structured data, legal directories, review platforms, and content they can confidently quote — to decide which firm, if any, to name. Here's what actually moves that needle.

  1. 1

    Add Attorney and LocalBusiness schema markup

    Structured data (schema.org's Attorney, LegalService, and LocalBusiness types) gives AI engines a machine-readable summary of your practice areas, location, and credentials — the same kind of explicit signal AI systems favor over inferring it from prose.

  2. 2

    Keep your profile complete and consistent across every legal directory

    A small group of directories — Avvo, Martindale-Hubbell, Super Lawyers, FindLaw, Justia, Chambers, and Best Lawyers — are cited disproportionately often in AI-generated legal answers. A missing, outdated, or inconsistent profile on any one of them is a missed citation opportunity.

  3. 3

    Claim and optimize your Google Business Profile

    It still feeds Gemini's grounded answers and remains a baseline trust signal every engine cross-references — practice areas, hours, service area, and up-to-date contact details all matter here.

  4. 4

    Publish genuinely helpful practice-area and FAQ content

    Write the direct, quotable answer to questions like "do I need a lawyer for X" or "how much does Y typically cost" — content built to be extracted and cited, not just to rank. Vague marketing copy gives an AI engine nothing concrete to quote.

  5. 5

    Build a steady, recent flow of client and peer reviews

    Reviews are one of the "4 R's" (Ratings, Reviews, Recognitions, Roots) researchers point to as consistently influencing which firms AI engines choose to name — and freshness matters, not just volume.

  6. 6

    Keep your firm name, address, and practice areas identical everywhere

    Inconsistent NAP (name/address/phone) data or differently-worded practice-area descriptions across your website, directories, and Google profile make it harder for an AI engine to confidently treat every mention as the same, trustworthy entity.

  7. 7

    Track what AI engines are actually saying — not just assume it

    Your Google ranking doesn't tell you whether ChatGPT recommends you. The only way to know is to ask the engines the real questions clients ask and check the answers directly, on a recurring basis, across every major engine.

The gap this creates

5W Research's 2026 Legal AI Visibility Report found 79% of lawyers already use AI internally for their own work — while a firm's visibility to AI engines, when a prospective client asks for a recommendation, has barely been touched. Most firms are optimized for a search engine that increasingly isn't where the first question gets asked anymore.

● Common Questions

Can AI engines like ChatGPT actually recommend a specific law firm?

Yes. When someone asks ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity a question like "best divorce lawyer in Denver" or "do I need a lawyer for a small claims case," these engines will often name specific firms or attorneys directly in the answer — usually pulled from legal directories, review platforms, and content the AI considers authoritative. Whether your firm is one of the names it gives depends on how consistently and clearly your firm's information appears across those sources, not on how well your website ranks in Google.

Is AI Visibility different from local SEO for law firms?

They overlap but are not the same thing. Local SEO optimizes for Google's Local Pack and organic rankings, driven by proximity, reviews, and backlinks. AI Visibility (GEO) optimizes for whether an AI engine's language model chooses to name your firm in a generated answer — which depends more on consistent structured data, directory presence, and content the model can confidently cite. A firm can rank well locally on Google and still be completely absent from ChatGPT's recommendations; see BG-008 for how common that gap is industry-wide.

How do I know if my law firm is being recommended by AI engines?

The only reliable way is to actually ask the AI engines the questions a prospective client would ask — across ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Perplexity, and Meta AI — and check whether your firm is named, how it's described, and which competitors show up instead.
