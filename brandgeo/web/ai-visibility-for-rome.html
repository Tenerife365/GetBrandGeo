AI Visibility in Rome: When One Engine Repeats Itself Word for Word

Like Berlin, Madrid, and Paris, Rome was tested bilingually — 4 categories, each asked once in Italian and once in English, across four AI engines. Rome's data looks different from every city tested before it: no single brand achieved strong cross-engine consensus. Instead, individual engines showed a striking pattern of talking to themselves.

For one category, Meta AI gave the exact same five restaurants, in the exact same order, in both Italian and English — the strongest language-stability result found anywhere in this research program. Perplexity showed a near-identical pattern for real estate agencies.

One engine, talking to itself

Meta AI's answer to "best restaurants for a business dinner in Rome" named the exact same five restaurants — La Rosetta, Il Pagliaccio, La Gensola, Il Convivio, La Campana — in the exact same order, whether asked in Italian or English. That's not a stable top pick reordered slightly; it's a fully reproduced ranked list. Perplexity showed a near-identical pattern for real estate agencies, naming essentially the same 9-agency set in both languages. Neither result was shared by any other engine — Claude, Gemini, and the other engine each gave their own, entirely different lists. Rome's AI Visibility landscape looks less like engines agreeing with each other and more like each engine having its own fixed, internally-consistent worldview.

The worst extraction gap of any city tested so far

Gemini returned zero structured competitors on 5 of the 8 Rome prompts, despite discussing brands in prose for most of them — a higher gap rate than New York (4 of 8) or any prior city. On the Termini-station hotel prompt specifically, both Gemini and Meta AI failed to extract any structured competitors in either language, even though Meta's own prose explicitly named specific hotels by name each time. Only Claude and Perplexity produced usable structured data for that prompt.

A data-quality note, not a finding

ChatGPT's collection failed on every Rome prompt this run (a technical error, `status: error`) — it isn't included in the tables above. Separately, one Perplexity response (the Italian business-dinner prompt) was unusually noise-corrupted — only 1 of 8 extracted entries was a genuine restaurant name, the rest were street addresses, generic labels like "address" and "contacts," and hotel names mixed in by the extraction pipeline. Both gaps are noted in the underlying data rather than treated as real findings about brand visibility.

What this means if your business is in Rome

  1. 1

    Don't assume one engine's answer predicts another's

    Rome's data shows engines can be highly self-consistent while disagreeing completely with each other. Being named by one AI engine doesn't tell you much about whether a different engine names you at all.

  2. 2

    A fixed, repeated answer is hard to break into — and worth knowing about early

    When an engine reproduces the exact same list in two languages, that's a strong signal the underlying answer is fixed rather than dynamically generated per query. If you're not on that list, it's worth understanding why before assuming a future query will go differently.

  3. 3

    Check whether an engine even sees your category at all

    Gemini and Meta both went completely silent on one Rome prompt, in both languages, despite Meta's own prose naming specific businesses. Before assuming poor visibility, confirm the engine actually produces a usable answer for your exact category.

  4. 4

    Re-run this regularly, not once

    These answers reflect what the engines say today, in each language, this week. Extraction quality and engine behavior both shift between runs — a one-time check tells you less than an ongoing one.

● Common Questions

What was the most unusual finding in the Rome data?

Meta AI gave a word-for-word identical, identically-ordered five-restaurant list for the business-dinner prompt in both Italian and English — the strongest single-engine language-stability result found anywhere in this research program. Perplexity showed a similar, near-identical pattern for real estate agencies.

Did every engine agree on the same brands in Rome?

No, and that's the headline difference from every other city tested so far. Rome showed no single brand with strong cross-engine consensus (3 or 4 of 4 engines agreeing). Instead, individual engines were internally consistent with themselves across languages, without agreeing much with each other.

Why is ChatGPT missing from this data?

ChatGPT's collection failed on every Rome prompt this run — a technical error, not evidence ChatGPT has zero visibility for Rome brands. This page reflects the engines that returned usable results: Claude, Gemini, Meta AI, and Perplexity. A future re-run will include ChatGPT once the collection succeeds.
