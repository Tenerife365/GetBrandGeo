Core GEO Disciplines

Generative Engine Optimization (GEO)

The practice of structuring content and brand signals so generative AI engines — ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Perplexity — synthesize a brand directly into the answers they generate, rather than simply linking to its site. GEO is the umbrella discipline the other five terms below sit under.

Read the full breakdown in BG-001, or see how GEO differs from traditional SEO specifically in BG-005.

Answer Engine Optimization (AEO)

Optimizing content to directly answer a specific question a user asks, in a format an AI engine can lift and cite verbatim. AEO is what makes a page "quotable" — a clear, self-contained answer instead of marketing copy the AI has to interpret and paraphrase.

More on the six disciplines in BG-001.

Large Language Model Optimization (LLMO)

Shaping how large language models describe and understand a brand at the model level, through consistent, accurate information across the sources those models are trained on and retrieve from — press coverage, structured business data, forums, and high-authority third-party mentions.

See BG-001 for how LLMO fits alongside the other disciplines.

Semantic SEO

Organizing content around topics, entities, and the relationships between them — not isolated keywords — so both search engines and AI systems understand what content actually means, not just which words appear on the page.

Discussed in BG-001.

Entity SEO

Establishing a brand as a distinct, verifiable entity inside the knowledge graphs AI engines draw from — with consistent naming, structured data (Schema.org, JSON-LD), and cross-referenced third-party confirmation of who the brand is and what it does.

See BG-001, and BG-010 for the practical implementation guide to the structured data this discipline depends on.

Technical SEO

Still the foundation. AI engines and the retrieval systems behind them — Perplexity's live web search, Gemini's Google index, Copilot's Bing index — still depend on crawlable, fast, well-structured sites with clean markup. Without it, none of the other five disciplines have anything to build on.

See BG-001.

Measurement & Product Concepts

AI Visibility

A brand's ability to be mentioned, recommended, or cited in answers generated by AI engines — including ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Perplexity, and Meta AI. It is distinct from SEO rankings and requires its own measurement and optimization strategy.

Full definition and context in BG-001.

AI Visibility Score

A composite measurement of a brand's AI Visibility across multiple weighted dimensions — recognition, knowledge, sentiment, accuracy, reach, and consistency — rather than a single mention count. A brand can be mentioned frequently and still score poorly if those mentions are inaccurate or negatively framed.

Why a single mention count isn't enough on its own: BG-012. Which KPIs actually matter: BG-015.

AI Visibility Gap

The difference between a brand's ranking in traditional search (Google, Bing) and its presence in AI-generated recommendations for the same queries. A brand can rank on page one of Google and still be completely absent from every AI engine's output, because AI engines don't index the web the way search engines do.

Original research on this gap: BG-001.

Share of Voice (in LLMs)

The proportion of AI-generated answers, within a given category or query set, that mention a specific brand relative to its competitors. Unlike traditional share-of-voice metrics built from media mentions, this is measured by actually querying the AI engines and counting who gets named.

Related: BG-012 on why AI Visibility and raw mention counts aren't the same thing.

Citation

An instance where an AI engine names a specific brand, source, or fact in its generated answer — sometimes with an attributed link (as Perplexity does for nearly every claim), and sometimes without one (as is common with ChatGPT and Meta AI's conversational answers).

What separates a cited brand from one that isn't: BG-007. How ChatGPT specifically decides what to cite: BG-009.

Cross-Engine Consensus

The degree to which multiple AI engines, queried independently on the same question, name the same brand or set of brands. High consensus means a brand's AI Visibility is durable across the whole category of engines a customer might use; low or zero consensus means each engine is effectively running its own separate reality.

See this measured directly, city by city, across BrandGEO's City Research program — for example Dublin, where two categories reached full consensus, versus Rome, where none did.

Sentiment & Framing

How an AI engine characterizes a brand when it does mention it — not just whether the brand appears, but whether it's described favorably, neutrally, or critically, and whether it's positioned as the recommended option or just one of several listed alternatives.

Technical Concepts

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

An architecture where an AI model retrieves relevant documents or web pages at query time and generates its answer grounded in that retrieved content, rather than relying only on what it learned during training. Perplexity's search-then-cite behavior and ChatGPT's web-search mode are both RAG in practice.

The mechanics in detail: BG-009.

Grounding

A model's decision, at query time, to run a live web search and base its answer on those results rather than answering from training data alone. Google's Gemini uses an explicit prediction classifier to decide whether a given query needs grounding — meaning the same brand can be visible on a grounded query and invisible on an ungrounded one, even from the same engine.

How this plays out for Gemini specifically: BG-006.

Knowledge Graph

A structured representation of entities and the relationships between them that an AI system uses to understand what a brand is, what category it belongs to, and how it connects to other entities. Being absent from the relevant knowledge graph is a common reason a genuinely well-known brand still gets zero AI citations.

Structured Data (Schema.org / JSON-LD)

Machine-readable markup embedded in a webpage that explicitly labels its content — an article, a product, a set of FAQ answers — for search engines and AI crawlers, using a shared vocabulary. Its actual effect on AI citation is genuinely contested in the research; some studies show a measurable citation boost, others find none.

The full implementation guide, including that open question: BG-010.

Zero-Click Search

A search interaction where the user gets their answer directly from the AI-generated response and never clicks through to a source website. As AI-generated answers become more common, a growing share of search sessions end without a single click — which is exactly why AI Visibility matters even for brands with strong traditional SEO rankings.

See BG-005 for the current zero-click rate in Google's AI Mode.

Content Freshness

How recently a piece of content was published or updated, used by several AI engines as a signal when deciding which sources to retrieve and cite. AI-cited content skews meaningfully more recent than content that merely ranks well organically.

The data behind this: BG-007 and BG-013.

Trust & Risk Concepts

Entity Recognition

Whether an AI engine's knowledge base treats a brand as a distinct, real entity — with a clear category, location, and purpose — rather than failing to recognize it at all. Brands that exist only as websites, without a broader knowledge footprint of press mentions and structured data, are often completely absent from AI answers regardless of how good their product is.

One of the three pillars of AI Visibility, covered in BG-001.

Trust Signals

The independent, third-party indicators — press coverage, authoritative citations, original data, clear authorship — that AI engines weigh when deciding how much confidence to place in a source. Brands that publish genuinely original research build this layer directly.

Which signals carry the most weight: BG-003.

Hallucination

An instance where an AI engine states something as fact that is fabricated, outdated, or unverifiable, rather than genuinely grounded in a real source. This is a real, ongoing risk in AI search — it's also why BrandGEO's own research and product data are built on genuinely collected results rather than assumed or invented figures, since the same accuracy problem that affects AI-generated answers can affect anyone publishing about AI search if they're not careful.
