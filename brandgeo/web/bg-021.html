How the 6-Dimension AI Visibility Score Is Actually Weighted

One number hides six separate measurements, unequally weighted on purpose. Here is the exact formula: what each dimension counts, why recognition outweighs consistency two and a half to one, and what happens to a mention with no provable rank.

One number, six measurements

The AI Visibility Score that shows up on a client's dashboard is a single number, and single numbers invite a natural question: what is it actually measuring, and can it be gamed? The honest answer is that it isn't one measurement, it's six, computed independently and folded together with unequal weights, in computeAuditScore. Publishing the exact formula is the only way to let that question actually get answered rather than taken on faith.

DimensionWeightWhat it actually measures
Recognition25%Raw mention rate across every prompt × engine pair with a result
Knowledge20%Average rank quality when mentioned, decaying with position
Sentiment15%Tone when mentioned: positive, neutral, or negative
Accuracy15%Share of mentions landing in the top 3 (or with no stated rank)
Reach15%Share of reachable engines that mention the brand at least once
Consistency10%Share of prompts where a majority of checked engines agree

Why recognition and knowledge outweigh the rest

Recognition, the plain rate of "did any engine mention the brand at all," carries the single largest weight at 25%. That ordering is deliberate: being mentioned is the precondition every other dimension depends on, so it is weighted as the precondition, not as one of six equal inputs. Knowledge, at 20%, is the second-heaviest, and it isn't binary either. A mention's quality decays with its rank, on a curve, not a cliff: Math.max(20, 100 - ((position - 1) / 4) * 80). Rank 1 scores 100, rank 3 scores 60, and the curve bottoms out at a floor of 20 by rank 5, a mention at rank 5 and a mention at rank 50 earn identical knowledge credit, because past a certain point in a real AI answer, the practical difference to a reader is negligible.

What happens to a mention with no provable rank

This is where the accuracy dimension connects directly to a decision we wrote about last week: our scorer refuses to guess a rank when an AI answer doesn't clearly state one, and returns null rather than a fabricated number. The accuracy dimension, which measures the share of mentions landing in the top 3, treats a null position as accurate by default, not as a penalty. A prose mention with no numbered list and no stated superlative doesn't get punished for lacking a rank we were honest enough not to invent. Being honest about not knowing costs nothing in the score; guessing wrong would have cost real accuracy.

Reach, consistency, and why the smallest weight matters most

Reach, covered in our previous piece, measures the share of engines that actually produced a usable result and mentioned the brand, explicitly excluding engines we failed to reach at all so our own outages can't manufacture a visibility gap. Consistency measures something different: whether a majority (60% or more) of the engines checked on a given prompt agree the brand belongs in the answer. It carries the smallest weight, 10%, on purpose. Consistency is the dimension most vulnerable to noise from a single unusual prompt or a single engine's idiosyncratic answer, and weighting it lightly keeps one outlier prompt from swinging the composite score disproportionately.

The full formula

aiScore = recognition×0.25 + knowledge×0.20 + sentiment×0.15 + accuracy×0.15 + reach×0.15 + consistency×0.10, each dimension itself a 0–100 value, rounded once at the end. Same formula, same weights, whether the score is a full client audit or a free public Instant Audit — a deliberate byte-for-byte match maintained by hand so the number on a prospect's free audit and the number on a paying client's dashboard mean exactly the same thing.

What this means when you're reading any composite AI visibility score

A single score is only as trustworthy as its weighting is disclosed. A vendor who publishes a headline number without the formula behind it is asking you to trust that mention rate, rank, sentiment, coverage, and consistency were all balanced sensibly, with no way to check. Ask what dimensions feed the number, what they're weighted, and whether a brand that's mentioned constantly but ranked poorly scores differently from one that's mentioned rarely but always in the top spot, because in an honest formula, it should.
