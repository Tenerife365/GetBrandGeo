We Turned Our City Research Into a Published Academic Paper

The dataset behind BG-016 and our seven city studies is now a formal empirical paper, published in the open with a permanent DOI. Here's what's in it, and why we published the data itself rather than just our conclusions about it.

Why we wrote this up as a paper, not just another article

Everything BrandGEO publishes on this site, including BG-016 and every one of our seven city research pages, comes from the same underlying activity: we run real buyer questions through real AI engines, using BrandGEO's own production collection pipeline, and we read what comes back. That's the same measurement the product itself performs for a paying client, just aimed at a research question instead of a single brand.

A blog post is the right format for a specific finding. It isn't the right format for the full dataset, the exact methodology, every disclosed limitation, and a citable, permanent record that someone else could actually check against. So we wrote the whole thing up properly: as a real empirical paper, with a data-and-methods section, a results table for all seven cities, an explicit definition of what we mean by "consensus," and a section that states plainly where the data is incomplete. Then we published it to Zenodo, a CERN-run open-access repository, which issued it a permanent, citable DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.21395598, under a CC BY 4.0 license.

Read the paper directly

The full text, the complete per-city results table, the methodology, and the disclosed limitations are open access at the permanent record: doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.21395598. No account, no paywall, no email required.

What's actually in it

Between July 9 and July 10, 2026, we ran 56 realistic, high commercial-intent buyer questions (the kind a real person might actually type into an AI assistant, like "which law firm should I use for an employment dispute in Dublin") across seven cities and five AI engines: ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Perplexity, and Meta AI. Two cities, London and Dublin, used eight distinct English prompts each. The other five, Berlin, Madrid, Paris, and Rome, used a paired design: the same four categories asked once in the local language and once in English, specifically so language could be studied as its own variable rather than tangled up with the city.

Of the 280 possible engine-level responses that design implied, 278 were actually recorded. We disclose this precisely rather than round it off: two rows, both Meta AI, were lost to a transient collection gap. A further 56 rows, all of them ChatGPT, came back as a recorded API error rather than a real answer, caused by a sustained account-level quota failure on the OpenAI account used for collection during the exact window we were gathering data. We exclude ChatGPT from the paper's analysis rather than quietly treat its absence as a real finding about ChatGPT. The paper's actual analytic dataset is 222 completed responses across four engines, and the paper says so on page one, not in a footnote.

The clearest finding, holding across every city we tested

Categories with a small number of well-documented, easily distinguished, frequently reviewed providers converge, often to full unanimity across every engine. Categories with a long tail of similarly-qualified, less differentiated providers, especially individually named professionals, almost never converge at all. This held independently in London, New York, and Dublin, three cities that don't share the bilingual design, so it can't be explained away as a language artifact.

New York gave the pattern its own sharp variant. Three long-established brokerages, Douglas Elliman, Corcoran, and Brown Harris Stevens, each reached three-of-four engine consensus for real estate, while individually named "star" agents in the exact same responses never reached agreement above a single engine. The same shape showed up in law: for startup incorporation, two nationally recognized firms, Fenwick & West and Cooley, beat every New York-specific boutique to three-of-four consensus.

Dublin added a distinct engine-level wrinkle worth knowing about on its own: Meta AI showed the highest overall consensus rate of any city (a corporate litigation prompt converged on Ireland's five largest law firms across every responding engine, and two separate prompts hit full 4-of-4 unanimity, on Monday.com and Revolut Business respectively) alongside a specific failure mode, in which Meta AI answered three genuinely different financial-advisory questions with nearly the same list of large institutions, regardless of whether the question explicitly asked for a boutique or independent option. Claude, Gemini, and Perplexity, asked the identical prompts, told the two scenarios apart cleanly. A single-engine visibility check would have missed that this was one engine's blind spot, not the real market.

Rome: zero cross-engine agreement, and the opposite result inside one engine

Rome is the one city in the whole dataset where not a single category reached even three-engine agreement. Every one of its four tested categories fragmented. And yet Rome also produced the single strongest within-engine stability result anywhere in the paper: for a business-dinner restaurant prompt, Meta AI gave an identical, identically ordered five-restaurant list in both Italian and English, an exact reproduction, not just an overlapping one. No engine agreed with any other engine in Rome. Each engine, on its own, was completely consistent with itself.

Paris: language changed the entire competitor set, not just the order

The paper's most theoretically significant result concerns wealth management in Paris. Asked in French, every responding engine converged on independent, boutique French patrimoine firms. Asked the identical question in English, they converged instead on major international private banking divisions, near-unanimous once combined, and almost entirely absent from the French-language runs. That's not the same list re-ranked by language. It's a different category of provider considered at all, purely as a function of which language the question was asked in. The same city also produced the paper's cleanest unanimous result overall: every engine that answered named Qonto as its top pick for French small-business banking, in both languages, with zero exceptions.

Why we published the disclosed limits along with the findings

Every dataset like this one has real, honest gaps, and this paper names them rather than smoothing them over. ChatGPT's exclusion is disclosed and explained. A Gemini-specific extraction pattern, where the model clearly named brands in prose but our automated pipeline didn't parse a structured list from bold or bulleted text, is disclosed and flagged separately from genuine cross-engine disagreement wherever it materially affects a result, most severely in Rome and New York. One Madrid response naming an apparently closed restaurant, and one Madrid response with a geographic mismatch, are both disclosed rather than quietly kept in as if they were clean data. One Rome response judged, on manual review, to be the most noise-corrupted single response in the whole program is called out by name.

This is the same standard BrandGEO already holds itself to on every research page we publish: a real, sourced, checkable claim, or a disclosed limitation, never an invented number and never a silently smoothed-over gap. A paper that can be checked is worth more than a paper that only sounds authoritative, and publishing under a CC BY 4.0 license means anyone, including the AI systems this whole paper is about, can freely read, quote, and cite it.
