## Title

Your Law Firm Might Be Winning on Google and Invisible on ChatGPT. You'd Never Know It.

## Subtitle / deck

A growing share of people now ask an AI chatbot who to hire before they ever
open Google. Here's what the data says about who AI engines actually
recommend, and a fast way to check where your firm stands.

## Body

Here's a test worth running before you read any further: open ChatGPT, and
ask it "who's the best divorce lawyer in [your city]." Then ask it "do I need
a lawyer for a small claims case." Then ask "[your firm's name] reviews, are
they any good."

If your firm's name didn't come up in any of those answers, you're not
imagining a gap. You're looking at one directly.

### The shift already happened

77-78% of legal search queries now trigger a Google AI Overview, the
highest rate of any industry vertical currently measured. 56% of AI chatbot
users say they've used ChatGPT directly to seek legal advice or guidance.
And roughly 1 in 5 consumers say they'd use ChatGPT specifically to research
which lawyer to hire, a number that skews higher among corporate and
general-counsel buyers, not lower.

Meanwhile, 79% of lawyers already use AI internally for their own work
(5W Research's 2026 Legal AI Visibility Report). The profession has adopted
the technology faster than it's noticed the technology now sits between it
and new clients.

### Why your Google ranking doesn't answer this

AI engines don't rank pages the way Google does. ChatGPT, Gemini, and
Perplexity pull from a narrower set of signals when deciding which firm, if
any, to name in a generated answer: structured Attorney and LocalBusiness
schema markup, consistency across a small set of legal directories (Avvo,
Martindale-Hubbell, Super Lawyers, FindLaw, Justia, Chambers, Best Lawyers), a
maintained Google Business Profile, and content written to be directly
quoted rather than just persuasive.

A firm can rank on page one of Google for its target keywords and still be
completely absent when a prospective client asks an AI engine the identical
question in plain language. That gap is common across local and professional
services. One measured study found 98.8% of local businesses are
effectively invisible to AI recommendations despite ranking well on Google.

### What this actually looks like, city by city

We ran this exact test, real prompts, across multiple AI engines,
independently, for London and Dublin, and the pattern is worth knowing if
you're trying to figure out whether your own market is "won" or still open.

For "which London law firm should I use for a commercial contract dispute,"
three different AI engines (Claude, Meta AI, and Perplexity), tested
independently, all converged on the same firm: Clifford Chance. That's a
category where one firm has already captured the AI narrative.

For "best employment law firms in London," the same three engines came back
with almost entirely different names each. No firm has captured that
narrative yet. The opportunity is still wide open for whoever gets their
signals right first.

Dublin showed the same split at an even starker level: for commercial
disputes, every engine we tested converged on the same five firms, Ireland's
established "Big Five" (A&L Goodbody, Matheson, McCann FitzGerald, Arthur
Cox, William Fry). For employment law, the same test returned a fragmented,
inconsistent set with no clear leader.

The takeaway isn't "some categories are hopeless." It's the opposite: a
fragmented category, where no firm currently owns the AI narrative, is
exactly the kind of gap that's still winnable, but only for a firm that
actually knows the category is fragmented, instead of assuming its Google
ranking is telling the whole story.

### What actually moves the needle

None of this requires abandoning your existing SEO work. It's additive, not
a replacement:

1. **Add Attorney and LocalBusiness schema markup.** A machine-readable
   summary of your practice areas, location, and credentials.
2. **Keep your directory profiles complete and consistent.** Avvo,
   Martindale-Hubbell, Super Lawyers, FindLaw, Justia, Chambers, and Best
   Lawyers are cited disproportionately often in AI-generated legal answers.
3. **Claim and keep your Google Business Profile current.** It still feeds
   Gemini's grounded answers and is a baseline cross-reference for every
   engine.
4. **Publish genuinely quotable practice-area content.** The direct answer
   to "do I need a lawyer for X" or "how much does Y cost," written to be
   extracted, not just to rank.
5. **Build a steady flow of recent reviews.** Ratings, reviews, recognitions,
   and roots (the "4 R's") are consistently cited as influencing which firms
   AI engines choose to name, and freshness matters as much as volume.
6. **Keep your name, address, and practice-area descriptions identical
   everywhere.** Inconsistent NAP data makes it harder for an AI engine to
   confidently treat every mention as the same trustworthy entity.
7. **Actually check what the engines are saying, not assume it.** The only
   way to know is to ask the real questions a prospective client would ask,
   across every major engine, on a recurring basis.
