It's not magic — it's millions of signals

One of the most common questions we hear is: "How does ChatGPT choose which company to recommend?"

The honest answer is that it's considerably more complex than PageRank ever was. There's no single link-counting algorithm to reverse-engineer. Instead, modern AI systems evaluate millions of signals working together to understand a brand, evaluate it against the competition, and decide whether it's worth recommending at all.

That process isn't a black box forever, though — it breaks down into a fairly consistent pipeline, and a fairly consistent set of signals. Understanding both is the difference between hoping AI recommends you and deliberately earning it.

The six-stage path from question to recommendation

Different AI engines — ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Perplexity, Microsoft Copilot, Meta AI, Grok — use different models, but they're solving the same problem the same general way: find the best answer. Here's what happens between the moment someone asks and the moment your brand does or doesn't get named.

1

Question

A user asks a question about a product, service, or company — often conversationally, not as a keyword string.

Example: "What is the best catering company in Bucharest?"

2

Knowledge & retrieval

The AI searches its training data and, for engines with live web access, the web itself, for relevant information and sources about the category and the brands operating in it.

Example: "What information is available about each brand?"

3

Reasoning

The model analyzes, compares, and evaluates the candidates it found based on relevance to the question, perceived quality, and context.

Example: "Which brands best match the user's intent?"

4

Sources & signals

It weighs trust signals: citations, reviews, expertise, freshness, authority, and consistency across everywhere the brand is mentioned — not just the brand's own website.

Example: "Which sources are credible and worth citing?"

5

Recommendation

The AI selects the brand or brands it believes are the most relevant and trustworthy match — this is the step where most brands quietly get filtered out.

Example: "Which brand provides the best overall value?"

6

Answer

The AI generates a response and recommends the brand(s) to the user — a single, direct answer that's often acted on without further comparison.

Example: "Here are the top recommended companies…"

Why this matters

A traditional search result gives someone ten blue links and asks them to do the comparing. This pipeline compresses all of that comparison into steps 3 through 5 — invisibly, before the user ever sees an answer. If your brand isn't built to win at those steps, it never reaches step 6.

The eight signals that matter most

Across the six stages above, the same categories of signal keep resurfacing. These are the inputs AI systems actually weigh when deciding who to cite and recommend:

  • Brand authority & reputation — how consistently and positively your brand is described across independent sources, not just your own marketing
  • High-quality, helpful content — original, specific content that directly answers real questions, rather than generic marketing copy
  • Entity recognition & relationships — whether AI knowledge graphs correctly identify who you are, what category you belong to, and how you relate to competitors and partners
  • Citations & mentions across the web — how often and how credibly you're referenced by other sites, publications, and platforms
  • Reviews & user sentiment — the volume and tone of what real customers say about you, aggregated across review platforms
  • Structured data & technical health — schema markup, crawlability, and clean technical foundations that make your content machine-readable
  • Freshness & consistency — whether your information stays current and doesn't contradict itself across different sources
  • Topical coverage & expertise — how thoroughly you cover your category, signaling genuine expertise rather than a single thin page

Being searchable isn't enough anymore

This is why AI Visibility matters. For two decades, being searchable — indexed, keyword-optimized, technically crawlable — was close to sufficient. It no longer is.

You also need to become understandable and trustworthy for AI: correctly recognized as an entity, consistently described the same way everywhere you're mentioned, and backed by the kind of third-party validation that no amount of on-site copywriting can substitute for.

"You can't control how AI thinks, but you can influence what AI knows and how it perceives your brand. That's the power of AI Visibility."
